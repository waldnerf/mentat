## Models & GenAI

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 1 | Models & GenAI | Foundation Model | A large model trained on broad data that can be adapted to many downstream tasks, serving as a reusable base rather than a single-purpose tool. Foundation models (including LLMs and multimodal models) shifted AI economics: one expensive base model can power thousands of applications through prompting or light adaptation. For executives, this concentrates capability in a few providers and makes model selection and vendor strategy a board-level concern. The key insight is leverage: build on foundations instead of training from scratch, while managing dependency, cost, and lock-in. | 9 | LLM, Frontier Model, Fine-Tuning | Google Cloud, Anthropic, NVIDIA |
| 2 | Models & GenAI | LLM | A foundation model trained on massive text corpora to understand and generate human language, and increasingly to reason and use tools. LLMs such as the GPT, Claude, and Gemini families are the workhorses of generative AI, powering copilots, chatbots, and agents. For executives, LLMs matter because they generalise across tasks with natural-language instructions, lowering the barrier to automation. Their limits are equally important: they can be confidently wrong (hallucinate), have knowledge cut-offs, and cost money per use. Choosing, grounding, and governing LLMs is now a core enterprise capability. | 10 | Foundation Model, Token, Context Window | Anthropic, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 3 | Models & GenAI | SLM | A compact language model with far fewer parameters than frontier LLMs, designed to run cheaply, quickly, and sometimes on-device or on-premise. SLMs trade some breadth of capability for lower cost, latency, and control. For executives they are a practical lever: many enterprise tasks (classification, extraction, routing, narrow assistants) do not need the largest model, and SLMs can cut inference costs dramatically while improving privacy and speed. The strategic pattern is a portfolio, using small models for high-volume routine work and large models where deep reasoning is required. | 7 | LLM, Tokenomics, Model Routing | Microsoft Learn, NVIDIA, IBM |
| 4 | Models & GenAI | Multimodal AI | Models that process and generate more than one type of data, such as text, images, audio, and video, within a single system. Multimodality lets AI read a chart, describe a photo, transcribe a call, or act on a screenshot, closing the gap between digital and physical information. For enterprises this expands the addressable use cases well beyond text: visual inspection, document understanding, voice interfaces, and video analysis. The executive implication is that previously siloed data types can be handled by one class of model, simplifying architecture but raising new governance questions around images, voice, and consent. | 7 | Foundation Model | Google Cloud, NVIDIA, Anthropic |
| 5 | Models & GenAI | Transformer | The neural network architecture, introduced in 2017, that underpins modern LLMs and most generative AI. Its attention mechanism lets a model weigh the relevance of every part of an input when producing each part of an output, enabling strong language understanding at scale. Executives do not need the internals, but should know that the transformer is the common foundation across today's leading models, which is why capabilities and limitations are broadly similar across vendors. Its scalability is what turned more compute and data into steadily better models over the past several years. | 5 | LLM, Context Window | NVIDIA, Google Cloud, IBM |
| 6 | Models & GenAI | Token | The unit of text a language model processes, roughly a word or word-fragment; models read and write in tokens, and pricing and limits are measured in them. Tokens matter to executives because they are the currency of AI: cost, speed, and context limits are all counted in tokens. A long document, a big prompt, or verbose output all consume more tokens and therefore more money and time. Understanding tokens demystifies AI billing and explains why prompt design, context management, and output length are genuine cost levers, not just technical details. | 6 | Context Window, Tokenomics, Prompt Engineering | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 7 | Models & GenAI | Context Window | The maximum amount of text (measured in tokens) a model can consider at once, spanning the prompt, retrieved data, and its own response. The context window is the model's working memory: anything outside it is invisible unless retrieved. Larger windows let models handle whole documents or long conversations, enabling richer analysis, but they raise cost and can dilute focus. For executives, context limits explain why enterprises pair models with retrieval and memory systems rather than pasting everything in, and why very long inputs are not automatically better. It is a practical constraint shaping architecture and cost. | 7 | Token, RAG, Agent Memory | Anthropic, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 8 | Models & GenAI | Embedding | A numerical representation (a vector) that captures the meaning of text, images, or other data so that similar items sit close together in mathematical space. Embeddings are how AI systems measure semantic similarity, powering search, recommendations, clustering, and retrieval. For executives, embeddings are the quiet workhorse behind enterprise search and RAG: they let systems find information by meaning rather than exact keywords. The practical value is turning messy unstructured data into something searchable and comparable, which is often the first step in making proprietary knowledge usable by AI. | 6 | Vector Database, Semantic Search, RAG | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, NVIDIA |
| 9 | Models & GenAI | Fine-Tuning | Adapting a pre-trained model to a specific domain, style, or task by training it further on curated examples. Fine-tuning bakes desired behaviour into the model, useful for consistent tone, specialised jargon, or narrow tasks. For executives, the key decision is fine-tuning versus retrieval: fine-tuning changes how a model behaves, while retrieval (RAG) changes what it knows. Fine-tuning costs more effort and can go stale, so it is best reserved for stable, well-defined needs. Techniques like LoRA have made it far cheaper, but many enterprise problems are still better solved by grounding a general model. | 7 | RAG | Microsoft Learn, Anthropic, NVIDIA |
| 10 | Models & GenAI | Frontier Model | The most capable, largest, and typically most expensive models at the leading edge of AI, produced by a small number of well-resourced labs. Frontier models set the ceiling of what is possible and often the benchmark against which others are judged. For executives they matter for high-stakes, complex work and as a signal of where capability is heading. They also concentrate strategic dependency and attract the most regulatory attention because of their potential for both value and harm. A sensible strategy uses frontier models selectively, not by default, given their cost. | 6 | Foundation Model | Anthropic, Google, NVIDIA |
| 11 | Models & GenAI | Open-Weight Model | Open-weight models publish their trained parameters so organisations can download, run, inspect, and adapt them, while closed models are accessed only through a provider's API. The choice shapes control, cost, privacy, customisation, and lock-in. Open-weight models offer data sovereignty, on-premise deployment, and no per-token vendor fee, at the cost of running your own infrastructure and lagging the frontier. Closed models offer the leading capability and managed convenience, with dependency and data-handling considerations. For executives this is a core architectural and risk decision, often resolved as a mix rather than an either-or. | 7 | Foundation Model, Sovereign AI | Google Cloud, Anthropic, NVIDIA |

## Prompting & Context

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 12 | Prompting & Context | Prompt | The instruction or input given to a generative model to elicit a response. The prompt is the primary interface to modern AI: natural language replaces code as the way to direct the system. For executives, the significance is accessibility, anyone can instruct AI, but also variability, results depend heavily on how requests are framed. Prompts are also an operational surface: they encode business logic, can leak sensitive data, and are a vector for attacks. Treating prompts as managed assets rather than throwaway text is a marker of AI maturity. | 6 | System Prompt, Prompt Engineering, Context Engineering | Anthropic, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 13 | Prompting & Context | System Prompt | A hidden, persistent instruction that sets a model's role, rules, tone, and boundaries for a whole session, separate from the user's message. The system prompt is where an enterprise encodes policy: what the assistant may do, refuse, or disclose. For executives it is a key control point, effectively the AI's job description and rulebook, and a governance asset that should be versioned and reviewed. It is also a security concern, since attackers try to extract or override it (system prompt leakage and prompt injection). Well-designed system prompts are central to consistent, safe behaviour. | 6 | Prompt, Guardrails, Prompt Injection | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, OWASP |
| 14 | Prompting & Context | Prompt Engineering | The discipline of designing effective instructions to get reliable, high-quality outputs from AI models, including structure, examples, and constraints. As models improve, crude prompting still yields crude results, so prompt engineering remains a practical skill that separates useful deployments from disappointing ones. For executives it matters as a capability to build: small teams who know how to instruct models well can unlock outsized value. It is also becoming systematised into templates, testing, and version control rather than ad hoc craft, which is how it should be treated at enterprise scale. | 6 | Context Engineering, Zero-Shot Prompting | Anthropic, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 15 | Prompting & Context | Zero-Shot Prompting | Two core prompting techniques: zero-shot asks a model to perform a task with no examples, relying on its general training; few-shot provides a handful of examples to steer format and behaviour. Few-shot often improves accuracy and consistency for specialised tasks without any retraining. For executives, these illustrate a powerful property of modern AI: behaviour can be shaped instantly with examples rather than costly fine-tuning, making experimentation cheap and fast. Knowing when a few good examples suffice, versus when deeper adaptation is needed, is a practical efficiency lever. | 5 | Prompt Engineering, Fine-Tuning | Google Cloud, Anthropic, Microsoft Learn |
| 16 | Prompting & Context | Chain-of-Thought | A prompting and model technique where the system works through intermediate reasoning steps before giving a final answer, improving accuracy on complex, multi-step problems. Chain-of-thought is the visible mechanism behind reasoning models and better analytical performance. For executives, it explains why some AI now shows its working and handles harder tasks, and why that comes with more time and cost per answer. It also aids trust and auditability, since the reasoning can be inspected. The trade-off is verbosity and expense, so it is applied selectively where correctness matters most. | 5 | Prompt Engineering | Google, Anthropic, NVIDIA |
| 17 | Prompting & Context | Prompt Chaining | Breaking a complex task into a sequence of prompts where each step's output feeds the next, creating a reliable multi-stage workflow rather than one large request. Chaining improves quality and control by decomposing problems, for example extract, then summarise, then classify. For executives it is an early form of AI workflow design and a bridge toward agents. The value is reliability and modularity: each step can be tested and monitored. It also signals a shift from one-shot prompting to engineered pipelines, which is how durable enterprise AI systems are built. | 5 | Agent Orchestration, Context Engineering | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 18 | Prompting & Context | Context Engineering | The practice of assembling the right information (instructions, retrieved data, memory, tools, and history) into a model's limited context window so it can perform reliably. Context engineering has largely superseded narrow prompt tweaking as the discipline that determines real-world AI quality. For executives it reframes the challenge: performance depends less on clever wording and more on feeding the model the right enterprise context at the right time. It ties directly to data readiness, retrieval, and memory, and is often the difference between a demo and a dependable production system. | 7 | RAG, Context Window, Agent Memory | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 19 | Prompting & Context | Grounding | Connecting a model's responses to authoritative, current, or proprietary sources so answers are based on verified information rather than the model's parametric memory alone. Grounding, usually via retrieval, is the primary defence against hallucination and outdated knowledge. For executives it is the difference between an assistant that sounds confident and one that is trustworthy on your data. Grounded AI can cite sources, respect access controls, and stay current, which is essential for enterprise use in regulated or high-stakes contexts. It is a cornerstone of reliable, auditable deployments. | 8 | RAG, Hallucination | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, IBM |

## Data & Knowledge

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 20 | Data & Knowledge | RAG | An architecture that retrieves relevant information from your own data sources and supplies it to a model at query time, so answers are grounded in current, proprietary knowledge rather than the model's training alone. RAG is the dominant pattern for enterprise AI because it makes general models useful on private data without retraining, while respecting access controls and citing sources. For executives it is often the highest-value, lowest-risk starting point: it reduces hallucination, keeps knowledge current, and keeps data governance intact. Quality depends on the underlying data, search, and chunking, so it is as much a data problem as an AI one. | 9 | Grounding, Vector Database | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 21 | Data & Knowledge | Vector Database | A database that stores embeddings and finds items by semantic similarity, enabling fast retrieval of the most relevant content for search, recommendations, and RAG. Vector databases are the retrieval engine behind grounded AI, letting systems find information by meaning across large corpora. For executives, they are a new but increasingly standard piece of the AI data stack, and a build-or-buy decision in their own right. The strategic point is that making proprietary knowledge AI-ready usually means embedding and indexing it, and the vector store is where that searchable knowledge lives. | 6 | Embedding, Semantic Search, RAG | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 22 | Data & Knowledge | Semantic Search | Search that finds results by meaning and intent rather than exact keyword matching, using embeddings to understand what a query is really asking. Semantic search dramatically improves how employees and customers find information across documents, tickets, and knowledge bases. For executives it is a concrete, high-value AI win: better findability of existing knowledge, often with immediate productivity impact. It is also the retrieval foundation of RAG. Combined with keyword search (hybrid search), it balances precision and recall, making enterprise search finally work the way people expect. | 6 | Embedding, RAG | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, IBM |
| 23 | Data & Knowledge | Chunking | The process of splitting documents into smaller passages so they can be embedded, retrieved, and fed to a model within its context limits. Chunking sounds mundane but strongly affects retrieval quality: chunks that are too large dilute relevance, too small lose meaning. For executives, chunking exemplifies why enterprise AI is a data-engineering discipline, not just model selection. Poor chunking is a common, hidden cause of disappointing RAG results. It is a reminder that AI performance often hinges on unglamorous data preparation choices well upstream of the model itself. | 4 | RAG, Embedding, Vector Database | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 24 | Data & Knowledge | Knowledge Graph | A structured network representing entities (people, products, places) and the relationships between them, giving AI an explicit, machine-readable map of how things connect. Knowledge graphs add precision and reasoning that free text alone lacks, and increasingly complement LLMs by grounding them in verified facts and relationships. For executives, they matter where accuracy and traceability are critical, such as compliance, supply chain, or customer 360 views. Combining knowledge graphs with generative AI (sometimes called GraphRAG) can reduce hallucination and improve explainability, at the cost of the effort to build and maintain the graph. | 5 | Ontology, RAG, Semantic Layer | Google Cloud, IBM, NVIDIA |
| 25 | Data & Knowledge | Semantic Layer | A business-friendly abstraction that defines common metrics, dimensions, and meanings on top of raw data, so people and AI query consistent, governed definitions rather than raw tables. A semantic layer is what lets AI answer questions like revenue or active customer the way the business defines them, not however a query happens to be written. For executives it is increasingly critical: it makes AI over enterprise data trustworthy and consistent, avoiding the chaos of conflicting numbers. It bridges messy data and reliable AI answers, and is becoming a foundational component of the analytics and AI stack. | 5 | Knowledge Graph | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, IBM |
| 26 | Data & Knowledge | Taxonomy | A hierarchical classification of concepts into categories and subcategories, giving an organisation a shared, structured vocabulary for content, products, and data. For executives, a good taxonomy underpins search, navigation, and consistent reporting, and reduces the ambiguity that causes AI to mis-classify or mis-retrieve information. It is unglamorous but compounding: agreed categories make every downstream AI and analytics effort more accurate. Taxonomies are the first step in turning tacit organisational knowledge into machine-usable structure, and the foundation on which richer ontologies and knowledge graphs are built. | 4 | Ontology, Knowledge Graph | IBM, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 27 | Data & Knowledge | Ontology | A formal model that defines entities and the relationships and rules among them, going beyond a taxonomy's simple hierarchy to capture how things actually connect. Ontologies give both people and AI an explicit, machine-readable map of a domain, improving precision, reasoning, and interoperability. For executives they matter where accuracy and traceability are critical, such as compliance, supply chain, or a unified customer view, and they underpin knowledge graphs and GraphRAG. The cost is the effort to build and maintain them, so they suit high-value domains where shared, rigorous meaning pays off. | 4 | Taxonomy, Knowledge Graph, Semantic Layer | IBM, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |

## Agents

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 28 | Agents | Agent | An AI system that can perceive a situation, reason about a goal, and take actions using tools to achieve it, rather than only responding to a single prompt. Agents automate well-scoped tasks end to end, for example retrieving a record, validating it, and updating a system. For executives, agents are the shift from AI that advises to AI that acts, which raises the value and the risk. They require permissions, oversight, and guardrails because they can take real actions. Agents are the current frontier of enterprise AI value and the focus of much 2026 investment. | 9 | Agentic AI, Tool Use, Agent Orchestration | IBM, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 29 | Agents | Agentic AI | AI systems composed of autonomous agents that pursue goals across complex, multi-step workflows with limited supervision, planning, adapting, and coordinating rather than executing single tasks. Where an AI agent automates a task, agentic AI drives outcomes at the workflow level. For executives this is the defining enterprise AI theme of 2026: the promise is automating whole processes, not just steps; the challenge is governance, reliability, and trust when software acts with autonomy. Agentic AI reshapes operating models and headcount assumptions, so it belongs on the strategy agenda, not just the technology roadmap. | 9 | Agent, Multi-Agent System | IBM, NVIDIA, Google Cloud |
| 30 | Agents | Autonomous Agent | An agent that operates with minimal human intervention, making decisions and taking actions independently toward a goal over extended tasks. Autonomy exists on a spectrum, from tightly supervised assistants to systems that run largely on their own. For executives, the degree of autonomy is a deliberate risk-and-control choice: more autonomy means more scale and speed but less human checkpoints. High-autonomy agents demand strong guardrails, monitoring, and clear accountability. The practical stance is to grant autonomy incrementally, matched to task reversibility and stakes, rather than as an all-or-nothing setting. | 6 | Agentic AI, Human-on-the-Loop | NVIDIA, IBM, Google Cloud |
| 31 | Agents | Copilot | An AI assistant embedded in a workflow or application that helps a human do their job faster, suggesting, drafting, and answering while the person stays in control. Copilots (in coding, office software, CRM, and more) are the most widespread enterprise AI pattern because they augment rather than replace, keeping a human in the loop. For executives they offer broad, lower-risk productivity gains and a gentle path to adoption. The value depends on integration and change management: a copilot only helps if it is in the flow of work and people actually use it. | 7 | Human-Agent Collaboration, Agent | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, IBM |
| 32 | Agents | Multi-Agent System | An arrangement where several specialised agents collaborate, each handling part of a task and coordinating results, to tackle problems too complex for one agent. Multi-agent systems mirror how human teams divide labour, for example a researcher, a writer, and a checker working together. For executives, they promise handling of sophisticated, cross-functional workflows, but add complexity in orchestration, cost, and failure modes (agents can compound each other's errors). Interoperability standards such as A2A are emerging to make agents from different vendors work together. The pattern is powerful but early, warranting pilots with tight scope and oversight. | 6 | Agent Orchestration, Agent-to-Agent (A2A) Protocol, Agentic AI | Google Cloud, NVIDIA, IBM |
| 33 | Agents | Agent Orchestration | The coordination layer that decides which agent, tool, or model handles each step of a task, manages the flow, and combines results into a reliable outcome. Orchestration is what turns a collection of agents and tools into a dependable system rather than a fragile chain. For executives it is the control point for reliability, cost, and governance in agentic AI: it enforces sequence, handles errors, and applies policy. Orchestration platforms are becoming a strategic part of the enterprise AI stack. The quality of orchestration often determines whether agentic pilots survive contact with production. | 6 | Multi-Agent System, Control Plane | IBM, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 34 | Agents | Planning | An agent's ability to break a goal into an ordered sequence of steps and decide how to accomplish it before or while acting. Planning is what separates a capable agent from a simple responder, enabling it to handle multi-step tasks like booking travel or resolving a support case. For executives, planning quality is a key determinant of how ambitious a task an agent can reliably handle. Weak planning leads to agents that wander or fail on complex work. Advances in reasoning models have improved planning, expanding the range of processes agents can realistically automate. | 5 | Agent Orchestration, Reflection | Google Cloud, NVIDIA, Anthropic |
| 35 | Agents | Reflection | An agent's capacity to review its own work, catch errors, and revise before finalising, effectively self-checking. Reflection improves reliability by letting agents notice mistakes a single pass would miss, at the cost of extra time and compute. For executives it is one of the techniques making agents trustworthy enough for real tasks, and a reason some agentic systems are slower but more accurate. It illustrates a broader shift toward AI that verifies itself rather than answering blindly. Reflection is not foolproof, so it complements, rather than replaces, human oversight and evaluation. | 4 | Planning | Anthropic, Google, NVIDIA |
| 36 | Agents | Tool Use | An agent's ability to call external tools, such as search, calculators, databases, or business applications, to extend beyond what the model knows and take real actions. Tool use is what makes agents genuinely useful: it lets them fetch live data, run calculations, and change systems of record. For executives it is the mechanism by which AI connects to the enterprise and acts on it, which is exactly why permissions and guardrails matter. The set of tools an agent can access defines both its usefulness and its risk surface, making tool governance a core control. | 7 | Function Calling, MCP | Anthropic, Google Cloud, IBM |
| 37 | Agents | Function Calling | The mechanism by which a model requests a specific software function with structured parameters, letting it trigger actions or fetch data through defined interfaces. Function calling is the technical bridge between generative AI and enterprise systems, turning natural-language intent into reliable API calls. For executives it is why AI can do things (create a ticket, query inventory, send an email) rather than just talk. It underpins agents and integrations. Because it grants the model the ability to invoke real operations, it must be tightly scoped and permissioned, which is a central design and governance concern. | 5 | Tool Use | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, Anthropic |
| 38 | Agents | Agent Memory | An agent's ability to retain information across steps and sessions, spanning short-term working memory within a task and long-term memory of past interactions, preferences, and facts. Memory is what lets agents maintain context, learn from experience, and personalise, moving beyond stateless single answers. For executives it is central to useful assistants and digital workers, but it introduces data governance questions: what is stored, for how long, who can see it, and how it is protected. Memory raises both capability and privacy stakes, so it should be designed with retention and access policy from the start. | 6 | Context Engineering, Context Window | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 39 | Agents | Skill | A discrete, reusable ability an agent can perform, such as summarising a document, querying a system, or completing a specific business action. Framing agent functionality as modular skills makes agents easier to build, govern, and reuse across the organisation. For executives, a skills library is a way to scale AI systematically: capabilities are developed once, vetted, and shared, rather than reinvented per project. It also aids governance, since each skill's permissions and behaviour can be reviewed independently. This modularity is emerging as a practical pattern for managing agent complexity at enterprise scale. | 4 | Agent, Tool Use, Agent Registry | Anthropic, IBM, Microsoft Learn |
| 40 | Agents | Agent Identity | The unique, verifiable identity assigned to an agent so its actions can be authenticated, authorised, attributed, and audited, much like an employee account. As agents take real actions across systems, they need identities distinct from the humans who deploy them. For executives, agent identity is foundational to security and accountability in agentic AI: without it, you cannot control what an agent may do or trace what it did. It is becoming a core requirement of identity and access management strategy, and a prerequisite for safely scaling autonomous agents across the enterprise. | 6 |  | Microsoft Learn, IBM, Google Cloud |
| 41 | Agents | Agent Registry | A managed catalogue of the agents operating in an organisation, recording what each does, its owner, permissions, and status. A registry provides the visibility and control needed to govern a growing fleet of agents, preventing the agent equivalent of shadow IT. For executives it is essential infrastructure as agent numbers grow: you cannot govern what you cannot see. Registries support discovery, reuse, audit, and decommissioning. They are an emerging but increasingly necessary component of enterprise AI governance, analogous to a service catalogue for the agentic era. | 5 | Agent Identity, Shadow AI | IBM, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 42 | Agents | Human-Agent Collaboration | Designing workflows where humans and AI agents work together, with clear handoffs, oversight, and division of labour that plays to each side's strengths. Rather than full automation, most enterprise value comes from thoughtful collaboration: agents handle scale and speed, humans handle judgement, exceptions, and accountability. For executives, the design of these interactions determines both productivity and trust, and is central to adoption and change management. Getting the human-agent boundary right, including when to escalate and how to keep humans meaningfully in control, is a leadership and operating-model question as much as a technical one. | 6 | Copilot, Human-in-the-Loop | Microsoft Learn, IBM, Google Cloud |
| 43 | Agents | MCP | An open standard, introduced by Anthropic in late 2024 and now backed across the industry, that standardises how AI applications connect to external tools, data, and systems, replacing custom, one-off integrations with a common protocol. MCP is often described as a universal connector for AI, using a client-server model so any compliant agent can use any compliant tool or data source. For executives it matters strategically: it reduces integration cost and lock-in, accelerates agent deployment, and is becoming foundational plumbing for enterprise AI. Adopting standards like MCP is a way to future-proof AI architecture. | 7 | Tool Use, Agent-to-Agent (A2A) Protocol | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, Google |
| 44 | Agents | Agent-to-Agent (A2A) Protocol | An open protocol, introduced by Google in 2025 and now governed by the Linux Foundation, that lets autonomous agents built by different vendors and frameworks discover, authenticate, and collaborate with one another. Where MCP connects agents to tools, A2A connects agents to agents. For executives it is the interoperability layer for multi-agent systems that must work across organisational and vendor boundaries, enabling cross-provider workflows without custom integration. It signals a maturing ecosystem where agents cooperate like services. Backing open interoperability standards reduces lock-in and positions the enterprise for a multi-agent future. | 5 | Multi-Agent System, MCP | Google, IBM, NVIDIA |

## Architecture

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 45 | Architecture | AI Gateway | A control layer sitting between applications and AI models that centralises routing, security, guardrails, cost tracking, caching, and observability across multiple providers. As enterprises use many models from many vendors, the AI gateway becomes the single governed entry point, the control plane for AI traffic. For executives it is a strategically important piece of the stack: it enforces policy and spend limits, prevents lock-in by enabling model switching, and gives visibility into who is using what. It turns sprawling, ungoverned model usage into a managed, auditable capability, which is essential for scaling AI safely. | 7 | Control Plane, Model Routing, AI FinOps | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, IBM |
| 46 | Architecture | Control Plane | The centralised layer for governing, securing, and observing all AI activity across an organisation, spanning models, agents, data access, policy, and cost. The control plane is the management brain of enterprise AI, providing consistent policy enforcement and visibility rather than fragmented, per-application controls. For executives it is where governance becomes operational: guardrails, permissions, spend, and audit converge. As agents and models proliferate, a coherent control plane is what keeps AI safe, compliant, and cost-effective at scale. It is emerging as a defining architectural concept for serious enterprise AI programmes. | 6 | AI Gateway, Agent Orchestration | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, IBM |
| 47 | Architecture | Model Routing | Automatically directing each request to the most appropriate model based on cost, capability, latency, or task type, rather than sending everything to one model. Routing sends simple queries to cheap, fast models and hard ones to powerful, expensive models, optimising the balance of quality and cost. For executives it is a direct lever on AI economics and performance, often cutting costs substantially without hurting outcomes. It also reduces vendor lock-in by making models interchangeable behind a common interface. Routing is a hallmark of a mature, cost-aware AI architecture rather than a naive single-model deployment. | 6 | AI Gateway, SLM, Tokenomics | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 48 | Architecture | AI Platform | An integrated environment that provides the tools, infrastructure, governance, and services to build, deploy, and manage AI applications and agents across an organisation. A platform approach replaces scattered, one-off AI projects with shared, governed capabilities, accelerating delivery and enforcing consistency. For executives it is a build-or-buy decision with major strategic implications for speed, cost, control, and lock-in. Platforms centralise model access, data connectivity, security, and monitoring. Choosing and standardising on a platform, whether from a hyperscaler, a specialist vendor, or built internally, is one of the more consequential enterprise AI architecture decisions. | 6 | Control Plane, MLOps | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 49 | Architecture | Sovereign AI | The capability to build and run AI using infrastructure, data, and models that remain under a nation's or organisation's own control and jurisdiction. Sovereign AI responds to concerns about data residency, regulation, security, and dependence on foreign providers. For executives, it drives decisions about where models run, which providers are acceptable, and whether to invest in on-premise or in-region capability. It is increasingly relevant amid geopolitical tension, export controls, and strict data-protection regimes. The trade-off is control and compliance versus the cost and capability advantages of using leading global providers, a balance each organisation must strike deliberately. | 6 | Open-Weight Model | NVIDIA, Google Cloud, IBM |
| 50 | Architecture | Agent Harness | The software scaffolding wrapped around a model that turns it into a working agent: the prompts, tools, memory, sandboxes, context policies, feedback loops, and recovery paths that let it actually finish tasks. Borrowed from software testing, where a harness runs code under controlled conditions, the term captures everything that is not the model itself. At its core is the agent loop: the model reads its context and decides an action, and the harness executes that action (running a tool, calling an API, writing to storage) and feeds the result back.
