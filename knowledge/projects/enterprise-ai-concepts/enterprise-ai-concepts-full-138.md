# Enterprise AI Concepts for C-Level Literacy

Source: knowledge/assets/concepts/enterprise-ai-concepts (canonical; this file is the source of truth for the enterprise AI concepts explorer)

Status: v1.0
Owner: Mentat
Last updated: 2026-07-23
Purpose: A curated, web-verified reference of the concepts a C-suite needs to reason about Enterprise AI in 2026. Each concept carries an executive definition (what it is, why it matters, an example where useful), an importance score (1 to 10) for board-level literacy, related concepts, and authoritative educational sources.

Scope note: This is a literacy set, not a technical encyclopedia. Concepts were rebalanced from a technical-heavy draft to add value, adoption, and governance vocabulary that boards actually debate, and to consolidate granular items. 138 concepts across 12 categories.

Categories: Foundations, Models & GenAI, Alignment, Prompting & Context, Data & Knowledge, Agents, Architecture, Operations, Governance & Trust, Security & Safety, Transformation & Value, Standards & Regulation.

Importance scoring: 10 = every executive must know it to function; 7 to 9 = core literacy for AI decisions; 5 to 6 = useful context; below 5 = specialist awareness.

## Foundations

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 1 | Foundations | Artificial Intelligence (AI) | Computer systems that perform tasks normally requiring human intelligence, such as understanding language, recognising patterns, making decisions, and generating content. AI is an umbrella term covering everything from rules engines to today's generative models. For executives it is less a single technology than a capability shift: work that once required human cognition can increasingly be automated or augmented. The strategic question is not whether AI works but where it creates durable advantage versus commodity cost. Treat AI as a portfolio of capabilities with different maturity, risk, and value, not a monolith. | 10 | Machine Learning, Generative AI, AI Strategy | IBM, Google Cloud, NIST |
| 2 | Foundations | Machine Learning (ML) | A branch of AI where systems learn patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed with rules. Given examples, an ML model infers a function that generalises to new cases, for example predicting churn from past customer behaviour. ML underpins most enterprise AI value today, from fraud detection to demand forecasting. It matters to leaders because performance depends on data quality and quantity, not just algorithms, which makes proprietary data a strategic asset. ML is also probabilistic: outputs are predictions with error rates, not guarantees, so decisions built on them need tolerance for uncertainty. | 9 | Deep Learning, Training, Data Product | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, NVIDIA |
| 3 | Foundations | Deep Learning | A subset of machine learning using neural networks with many layers to learn complex patterns directly from raw data such as images, audio, and text. Deep learning removed much of the manual feature engineering that older ML required, which is why it powers modern speech recognition, computer vision, and large language models. For executives, the key implication is scale: deep learning improves with more data and compute, driving both breakthrough capability and significant infrastructure cost. It is powerful but opaque, so explainability and governance become harder as models grow deeper. | 8 | Neural Network, Foundation Model, GPU | NVIDIA, IBM, Google Cloud |
| 4 | Foundations | Neural Network | A computing structure loosely inspired by the brain, made of interconnected nodes (neurons) organised in layers that transform inputs into outputs by adjusting numerical weights during training. Neural networks are the engine beneath deep learning and generative AI. Executives do not need the mathematics, but should understand two things: capability emerges from scale (more parameters and data), and behaviour is learned rather than specified, so networks can be accurate yet unpredictable at the edges. This learned, statistical nature is why testing, monitoring, and guardrails matter more than with traditional software. | 7 | Deep Learning, Transformer, Training | NVIDIA, IBM, Google Cloud |
| 5 | Foundations | Natural Language Processing (NLP) | The field enabling computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language. NLP spans translation, sentiment analysis, summarisation, search, and chatbots. Large language models have dramatically raised NLP performance, turning what was once brittle and narrow into broadly capable text understanding. For enterprises this unlocks value in any language-heavy process: contracts, support tickets, research, compliance review. The executive takeaway is that unstructured text, historically hard to use at scale, is now a workable data source. NLP quality still varies by language, domain, and jargon, so specialist domains may need tuning or grounding. | 6 | Large Language Model, Semantic Search, Grounding | Google Cloud, IBM, Microsoft Learn |
| 6 | Foundations | Computer Vision | AI that interprets visual information from images and video, enabling tasks such as object detection, quality inspection, medical imaging analysis, and document scanning. Modern multimodal models combine vision with language, so systems can now describe, reason about, and act on what they see. For executives, computer vision matters wherever physical or visual processes exist: manufacturing defect detection, retail shelf monitoring, insurance claims from photos. Value depends on representative training images and controlled conditions; performance can degrade with lighting, angles, or rare cases, so human review remains important in high-stakes settings. | 6 | Multimodal AI, Deep Learning, Neural Network | NVIDIA, Google Cloud, IBM |
| 7 | Foundations | Training (Pre-training) | The compute-intensive process of teaching a model by exposing it to large datasets so it adjusts its internal parameters to capture patterns. Pre-training builds a foundation model's general capability from vast text or multimodal data; it is expensive, done infrequently, and dominated by a few large providers. Executives should distinguish training (building the model) from inference (using it), because their cost profiles differ sharply. Most enterprises will not train foundation models from scratch; they consume pre-trained models and adapt them. Understanding this shapes build-versus-buy and vendor dependency decisions. | 7 | Fine-Tuning, Inference, Foundation Model | NVIDIA, Google Cloud, Anthropic |
| 8 | Foundations | Inference | The act of running a trained model to produce an output, for example generating an answer or classifying an image. Inference is where AI delivers value in production, and, unlike one-off training, it recurs with every request, so its cost scales with usage. For executives this is central to economics: a successful AI product can incur large, ongoing inference bills. Managing latency, throughput, and cost per inference becomes an operational discipline. Choices such as smaller models, caching, and routing exist largely to control inference cost while preserving quality. | 8 | Training, Inference Cost, Latency | NVIDIA, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 9 | Foundations | GPU / Accelerated Computing | Graphics processing units and related accelerators are specialised chips that perform the massively parallel calculations AI training and inference require, far faster than general-purpose CPUs. GPUs are the strategic bottleneck of the AI era: supply, cost, and access shape who can build and run advanced AI. For executives, this drives cloud-versus-on-premise decisions, vendor negotiations, and capital planning. It also underpins geopolitical dynamics such as export controls and sovereign AI investment. The practical implication is that compute is a scarce, expensive input, so efficiency (model size, utilisation) is a real lever on both cost and sustainability. | 7 | Inference, AI Factory, Sovereign AI | NVIDIA, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |

## Models & GenAI

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 10 | Models & GenAI | Generative AI (GenAI) | AI that creates new content, including text, images, code, audio, and video, rather than only classifying or predicting. Generative AI, driven by foundation models, is the wave that moved AI from back-office prediction to front-line content and interaction. For executives it is the most consequential recent shift because it touches knowledge work directly: drafting, coding, analysis, and customer interaction. The opportunity is broad productivity gain; the risks are accuracy, intellectual property, and misuse. GenAI is a general-purpose capability, so the leadership challenge is prioritising where it creates real value versus novelty. | 10 | Foundation Model, Large Language Model, AI Strategy | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, NVIDIA |
| 11 | Models & GenAI | Foundation Model | A large model trained on broad data that can be adapted to many downstream tasks, serving as a reusable base rather than a single-purpose tool. Foundation models (including LLMs and multimodal models) shifted AI economics: one expensive base model can power thousands of applications through prompting or light adaptation. For executives, this concentrates capability in a few providers and makes model selection and vendor strategy a board-level concern. The key insight is leverage: build on foundations instead of training from scratch, while managing dependency, cost, and lock-in. | 9 | Large Language Model, Frontier Model, Fine-Tuning | Google Cloud, Anthropic, NVIDIA |
| 12 | Models & GenAI | LLM | A foundation model trained on massive text corpora to understand and generate human language, and increasingly to reason and use tools. LLMs such as the GPT, Claude, and Gemini families are the workhorses of generative AI, powering copilots, chatbots, and agents. For executives, LLMs matter because they generalise across tasks with natural-language instructions, lowering the barrier to automation. Their limits are equally important: they can be confidently wrong (hallucinate), have knowledge cut-offs, and cost money per use. Choosing, grounding, and governing LLMs is now a core enterprise capability. | 10 | Foundation Model, Token, Context Window | Anthropic, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 13 | Models & GenAI | SML | A compact language model with far fewer parameters than frontier LLMs, designed to run cheaply, quickly, and sometimes on-device or on-premise. SLMs trade some breadth of capability for lower cost, latency, and control. For executives they are a practical lever: many enterprise tasks (classification, extraction, routing, narrow assistants) do not need the largest model, and SLMs can cut inference costs dramatically while improving privacy and speed. The strategic pattern is a portfolio, using small models for high-volume routine work and large models where deep reasoning is required. | 7 | Large Language Model, Inference Cost, Model Routing | Microsoft Learn, NVIDIA, IBM |
| 14 | Models & GenAI | Multimodal AI | Models that process and generate more than one type of data, such as text, images, audio, and video, within a single system. Multimodality lets AI read a chart, describe a photo, transcribe a call, or act on a screenshot, closing the gap between digital and physical information. For enterprises this expands the addressable use cases well beyond text: visual inspection, document understanding, voice interfaces, and video analysis. The executive implication is that previously siloed data types can be handled by one class of model, simplifying architecture but raising new governance questions around images, voice, and consent. | 7 | Computer Vision, Foundation Model, Computer Use | Google Cloud, NVIDIA, Anthropic |
| 15 | Models & GenAI | Transformer | The neural network architecture, introduced in 2017, that underpins modern LLMs and most generative AI. Its attention mechanism lets a model weigh the relevance of every part of an input when producing each part of an output, enabling strong language understanding at scale. Executives do not need the internals, but should know that the transformer is the common foundation across today's leading models, which is why capabilities and limitations are broadly similar across vendors. Its scalability is what turned more compute and data into steadily better models over the past several years. | 5 | Neural Network, Large Language Model, Context Window | NVIDIA, Google Cloud, IBM |
| 16 | Models & GenAI | Token | The unit of text a language model processes, roughly a word or word-fragment; models read and write in tokens, and pricing and limits are measured in them. Tokens matter to executives because they are the currency of AI: cost, speed, and context limits are all counted in tokens. A long document, a big prompt, or verbose output all consume more tokens and therefore more money and time. Understanding tokens demystifies AI billing and explains why prompt design, context management, and output length are genuine cost levers, not just technical details. | 6 | Context Window, Inference Cost, Prompt Engineering | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 17 | Models & GenAI | Context Window | The maximum amount of text (measured in tokens) a model can consider at once, spanning the prompt, retrieved data, and its own response. The context window is the model's working memory: anything outside it is invisible unless retrieved. Larger windows let models handle whole documents or long conversations, enabling richer analysis, but they raise cost and can dilute focus. For executives, context limits explain why enterprises pair models with retrieval and memory systems rather than pasting everything in, and why very long inputs are not automatically better. It is a practical constraint shaping architecture and cost. | 7 | Token, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Agent Memory | Anthropic, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 18 | Models & GenAI | Embedding | A numerical representation (a vector) that captures the meaning of text, images, or other data so that similar items sit close together in mathematical space. Embeddings are how AI systems measure semantic similarity, powering search, recommendations, clustering, and retrieval. For executives, embeddings are the quiet workhorse behind enterprise search and RAG: they let systems find information by meaning rather than exact keywords. The practical value is turning messy unstructured data into something searchable and comparable, which is often the first step in making proprietary knowledge usable by AI. | 6 | Vector Database, Semantic Search, Retrieval-Augmented Generation | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, NVIDIA |
| 19 | Models & GenAI | Fine-Tuning | Adapting a pre-trained model to a specific domain, style, or task by training it further on curated examples. Fine-tuning bakes desired behaviour into the model, useful for consistent tone, specialised jargon, or narrow tasks. For executives, the key decision is fine-tuning versus retrieval: fine-tuning changes how a model behaves, while retrieval (RAG) changes what it knows. Fine-tuning costs more effort and can go stale, so it is best reserved for stable, well-defined needs. Techniques like LoRA have made it far cheaper, but many enterprise problems are still better solved by grounding a general model. | 7 | LoRA / PEFT, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Training | Microsoft Learn, Anthropic, NVIDIA |
| 20 | Models & GenAI | LoRA / PEFT | Parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), that adapt a large model by training only a small set of additional parameters rather than the whole model. This slashes the cost, time, and hardware needed to customise models and lets one base model serve many tuned variants cheaply. For executives, PEFT is why bespoke model behaviour has become economically viable for more organisations, and why running multiple specialised adapters on shared infrastructure is now practical. It lowers the barrier to differentiation without the expense of full fine-tuning or training. | 5 | Fine-Tuning, Small Language Model, Inference Cost | NVIDIA, Microsoft Learn, IBM |
| 21 | Models & GenAI | Model Distillation | Training a smaller, cheaper model to mimic the behaviour of a larger, more capable one, transferring much of the capability at a fraction of the running cost. Distillation is a key technique behind efficient small models and on-device AI. For executives it matters as a cost-and-performance lever: a distilled model can deliver most of the value of a frontier model for a specific task at far lower inference cost and latency. It also raises intellectual property and licensing questions when distilling from third-party models, which governance should address. | 5 | Small Language Model, Quantization, Inference Cost | NVIDIA, Google Cloud, IBM |
| 22 | Models & GenAI | Quantization | Reducing the numerical precision of a model's parameters (for example from 16-bit to 4-bit) to shrink its size and speed up inference, with minimal loss of quality. Quantization lets large models run on cheaper hardware or on-device, cutting cost and enabling edge deployment. For executives it is a technical efficiency lever that expands where AI can run and lowers operating cost. The trade-off is a small, usually acceptable, drop in accuracy. Together with distillation, quantization is part of the toolkit that makes capable AI affordable at enterprise scale. | 4 | Model Distillation, Inference Cost, GPU | NVIDIA, Microsoft Learn, IBM |
| 23 | Models & GenAI | Mixture of Experts (MoE) | A model architecture that contains many specialised sub-networks (experts) and activates only the relevant few for each input, delivering the capability of a very large model at a fraction of the compute per query. MoE is a major reason recent frontier models are both more capable and more efficient. For executives, the relevance is indirect but real: MoE improves the cost-performance frontier, meaning better models for less money over time. It is one of the architectural advances quietly shaping the economics and pace of AI improvement. | 4 | Frontier Model, Inference Cost, Transformer | NVIDIA, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 24 | Models & GenAI | Reasoning Model | A class of models trained to work through problems step by step before answering, spending extra computation to plan, check, and refine their reasoning. Reasoning models markedly improve performance on complex tasks such as maths, coding, analysis, and multi-step planning, at the cost of higher latency and price. For executives, they represent a distinct tier: use them for hard, high-value problems where accuracy justifies cost, and cheaper models for routine work. They are central to more capable agents, and their emergence shifted the frontier from raw knowledge to deliberate problem-solving. | 8 | Test-Time Compute, Chain-of-Thought, Agentic AI | Anthropic, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 25 | Models & GenAI | Test-Time (Inference-Time) Compute | The technique of improving answers by having a model spend more computation while responding, for example by thinking longer, exploring multiple solution paths, or self-checking, rather than by building a bigger model. Test-time compute is the mechanism behind reasoning models and a new axis of scaling: quality can be bought per query, not just per training run. For executives this reframes cost: harder questions can be given more compute on demand, so spending becomes a tunable dial between speed, cost, and accuracy. It also means capability can improve without retraining. | 6 | Reasoning Model, Chain-of-Thought, Inference Cost | Anthropic, NVIDIA, Google Cloud |
| 26 | Models & GenAI | Frontier Model | The most capable, largest, and typically most expensive models at the leading edge of AI, produced by a small number of well-resourced labs. Frontier models set the ceiling of what is possible and often the benchmark against which others are judged. For executives they matter for high-stakes, complex work and as a signal of where capability is heading. They also concentrate strategic dependency and attract the most regulatory attention because of their potential for both value and harm. A sensible strategy uses frontier models selectively, not by default, given their cost. | 6 | Foundation Model, Reasoning Model, AI Governance | Anthropic, Google, NVIDIA |
| 27 | Models & GenAI | Open-Weight Model | Open-weight models publish their trained parameters so organisations can download, run, inspect, and adapt them, while closed models are accessed only through a provider's API. The choice shapes control, cost, privacy, customisation, and lock-in. Open-weight models offer data sovereignty, on-premise deployment, and no per-token vendor fee, at the cost of running your own infrastructure and lagging the frontier. Closed models offer the leading capability and managed convenience, with dependency and data-handling considerations. For executives this is a core architectural and risk decision, often resolved as a mix rather than an either-or. | 7 | Foundation Model, Sovereign AI, Build vs Buy | Google Cloud, Anthropic, NVIDIA |

## Alignment

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 28 | Alignment | Model Alignment | The practice of making a model's behaviour match human intentions, values, and safety expectations, so it is helpful, honest, and avoids harmful outputs. Alignment is why a raw model trained only to predict text can become a well-behaved assistant. For executives it matters because alignment is never perfect: models can be manipulated, can misjudge context, or can reflect training biases. Understanding alignment frames why guardrails, testing, and oversight are necessary, and why vendor choices carry implicit value judgements. Alignment quality is a genuine differentiator between providers and a legitimate procurement criterion. | 7 | RLHF, Constitutional AI, Responsible AI | Anthropic, NIST, Google |
| 29 | Alignment | RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) | A training method where human preferences guide a model toward more helpful and appropriate responses, by rewarding outputs people rate highly. RLHF is a large part of why modern assistants feel useful and safe rather than erratic. For executives, the key point is that model behaviour is shaped by human judgement at scale, which introduces both quality and subjectivity: whose preferences, and which values, are embedded. It also explains why models improve on helpfulness and tone over time. RLHF is foundational to alignment but does not eliminate bias or error. | 5 | Model Alignment, Constitutional AI, Fine-Tuning | Anthropic, Google, IBM |
| 30 | Alignment | Constitutional AI | An alignment approach, developed by Anthropic, that trains a model to follow an explicit set of written principles (a constitution) so it can critique and improve its own responses with less direct human labelling. It aims to make model values more transparent and consistent. For executives, Constitutional AI illustrates that model behaviour can be governed by stated principles rather than opaque preference data, which is attractive for auditability and trust. It is one example of the broader trend toward documented, inspectable value systems in AI, relevant when evaluating vendor safety practices. | 4 | Model Alignment, RLHF, Responsible AI | Anthropic |
| 31 | Alignment | Synthetic Data | Artificially generated data, often produced by AI models, used to train or test other models when real data is scarce, sensitive, or imbalanced. Synthetic data can protect privacy, fill gaps, and reduce cost, for example generating rare fraud cases or anonymised records. For executives it is both an opportunity and a caution: it can accelerate development and reduce compliance exposure, but poor synthetic data can amplify bias or degrade quality (model collapse). Used well, it is a strategic asset for organisations that lack large proprietary datasets; used carelessly, it introduces subtle risk. | 5 | Training, Data Product, Bias & Fairness | NVIDIA, Google Cloud, IBM |

## Prompting & Context

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 32 | Prompting & Context | Prompt | The instruction or input given to a generative model to elicit a response. The prompt is the primary interface to modern AI: natural language replaces code as the way to direct the system. For executives, the significance is accessibility, anyone can instruct AI, but also variability, results depend heavily on how requests are framed. Prompts are also an operational surface: they encode business logic, can leak sensitive data, and are a vector for attacks. Treating prompts as managed assets rather than throwaway text is a marker of AI maturity. | 6 | System Prompt, Prompt Engineering, Context Engineering | Anthropic, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 33 | Prompting & Context | System Prompt | A hidden, persistent instruction that sets a model's role, rules, tone, and boundaries for a whole session, separate from the user's message. The system prompt is where an enterprise encodes policy: what the assistant may do, refuse, or disclose. For executives it is a key control point, effectively the AI's job description and rulebook, and a governance asset that should be versioned and reviewed. It is also a security concern, since attackers try to extract or override it (system prompt leakage and prompt injection). Well-designed system prompts are central to consistent, safe behaviour. | 6 | Prompt, Guardrails, Prompt Injection | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, OWASP |
| 34 | Prompting & Context | Prompt Engineering | The discipline of designing effective instructions to get reliable, high-quality outputs from AI models, including structure, examples, and constraints. As models improve, crude prompting still yields crude results, so prompt engineering remains a practical skill that separates useful deployments from disappointing ones. For executives it matters as a capability to build: small teams who know how to instruct models well can unlock outsized value. It is also becoming systematised into templates, testing, and version control rather than ad hoc craft, which is how it should be treated at enterprise scale. | 6 | Prompt Template, Context Engineering, Zero-Shot / Few-Shot Prompting | Anthropic, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 35 | Prompting & Context | Zero-Shot Prompting | Two core prompting techniques: zero-shot asks a model to perform a task with no examples, relying on its general training; few-shot provides a handful of examples to steer format and behaviour. Few-shot often improves accuracy and consistency for specialised tasks without any retraining. For executives, these illustrate a powerful property of modern AI: behaviour can be shaped instantly with examples rather than costly fine-tuning, making experimentation cheap and fast. Knowing when a few good examples suffice, versus when deeper adaptation is needed, is a practical efficiency lever. | 5 | Prompt Engineering, Fine-Tuning, Prompt Template | Google Cloud, Anthropic, Microsoft Learn |
| 36 | Prompting & Context | Chain-of-Thought | A prompting and model technique where the system works through intermediate reasoning steps before giving a final answer, improving accuracy on complex, multi-step problems. Chain-of-thought is the visible mechanism behind reasoning models and better analytical performance. For executives, it explains why some AI now shows its working and handles harder tasks, and why that comes with more time and cost per answer. It also aids trust and auditability, since the reasoning can be inspected. The trade-off is verbosity and expense, so it is applied selectively where correctness matters most. | 5 | Reasoning Model, Test-Time Compute, Prompt Engineering | Google, Anthropic, NVIDIA |
| 37 | Prompting & Context | Prompt Chaining | Breaking a complex task into a sequence of prompts where each step's output feeds the next, creating a reliable multi-stage workflow rather than one large request. Chaining improves quality and control by decomposing problems, for example extract, then summarise, then classify. For executives it is an early form of AI workflow design and a bridge toward agents. The value is reliability and modularity: each step can be tested and monitored. It also signals a shift from one-shot prompting to engineered pipelines, which is how durable enterprise AI systems are built. | 5 | Agent Orchestration, Workflow Automation, Context Engineering | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 38 | Prompting & Context | Context Engineering | The practice of assembling the right information (instructions, retrieved data, memory, tools, and history) into a model's limited context window so it can perform reliably. Context engineering has largely superseded narrow prompt tweaking as the discipline that determines real-world AI quality. For executives it reframes the challenge: performance depends less on clever wording and more on feeding the model the right enterprise context at the right time. It ties directly to data readiness, retrieval, and memory, and is often the difference between a demo and a dependable production system. | 7 | Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Context Window, Agent Memory | Anthropic, Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud |
| 39 | Prompting & Context | Structured Outputs | Constraining a model to return data in a strict, predefined format such as JSON that conforms to a schema, so its output can be reliably consumed by other software. Structured outputs are what turn free-form generation into dependable system integration, enabling AI to populate databases, trigger APIs, and drive workflows. For executives, this is the unglamorous but essential capability that lets AI plug into existing systems rather than just chat. It reduces errors and engineering effort, and is foundational to function calling, agents, and any automation where the AI's output must be machine-readable. | 5 | Function Calling, Tool Use, Enterprise Integration | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, Anthropic |
| 40 | Prompting & Context | Prompt Caching | Reusing the model's processing of repeated prompt content (such as a long system prompt or document) across requests, so it is not recomputed each time, cutting cost and latency substantially. Prompt caching is a practical efficiency lever for high-volume applications with stable context. For executives it is one of several mechanisms that make production AI economical: the same capability can cost far less with caching in place. It rewards good design (stable, reusable context) and is part of the operational toolkit for managing inference cost at scale. | 4 | Inference Cost, Context Window, Token | Anthropic, Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn |
| 41 | Prompting & Context | Grounding | Connecting a model's responses to authoritative, current, or proprietary sources so answers are based on verified information rather than the model's parametric memory alone. Grounding, usually via retrieval, is the primary defence against hallucination and outdated knowledge. For executives it is the difference between an assistant that sounds confident and one that is trustworthy on your data. Grounded AI can cite sources, respect access controls, and stay current, which is essential for enterprise use in regulated or high-stakes contexts. It is a cornerstone of reliable, auditable deployments. | 8 | Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Hallucination, Enterprise Search | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, IBM |

## Data & Knowledge

| ID | Category | Concept | Executive Definition | Importance | Related Concepts | Sources |
|----|----------|---------|----------------------|:----------:|------------------|---------|
| 42 | Data & Knowledge | Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) | An architecture that retrieves relevant information from your own data sources and supplies it to a model at query time, so answers are grounded in current, proprietary knowledge rather than the model's training alone. RAG is the dominant pattern for enterprise AI because it makes general models useful on private data without retraining, while respecting access controls and citing sources. For executives it is often the highest-value, lowest-risk starting point: it reduces hallucination, keeps knowledge current, and keeps data governance intact. Quality depends on the underlying data, search, and chunking, so it is as much a data problem as an AI one. | 9 | Grounding, Vector Database, Enterprise Search | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 43 | Data & Knowledge | Vector Database | A database that stores embeddings and finds items by semantic similarity, enabling fast retrieval of the most relevant content for search, recommendations, and RAG. Vector databases are the retrieval engine behind grounded AI, letting systems find information by meaning across large corpora. For executives, they are a new but increasingly standard piece of the AI data stack, and a build-or-buy decision in their own right. The strategic point is that making proprietary knowledge AI-ready usually means embedding and indexing it, and the vector store is where that searchable knowledge lives. | 6 | Embedding, Semantic Search, Retrieval-Augmented Generation | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 44 | Data & Knowledge | Semantic Search | Search that finds results by meaning and intent rather than exact keyword matching, using embeddings to understand what a query is really asking. Semantic search dramatically improves how employees and customers find information across documents, tickets, and knowledge bases. For executives it is a concrete, high-value AI win: better findability of existing knowledge, often with immediate productivity impact. It is also the retrieval foundation of RAG. Combined with keyword search (hybrid search), it balances precision and recall, making enterprise search finally work the way people expect. | 6 | Embedding, Enterprise Search, Retrieval-Augmented Generation | Google Cloud, Microsoft Learn, IBM |
| 45 | Data & Knowledge | Chunking | The process of splitting documents into smaller passages so they can be embedded, retrieved, and fed to a model within its context limits. Chunking sounds mundane but strongly affects retrieval quality: chunks that are too large dilute relevance, too small lose meaning. For executives, chunking exemplifies why enterprise AI is a data-engineering discipline, not just model selection. Poor chunking is a common, hidden cause of disappointing RAG results. It is a reminder that AI performance often hinges on unglamorous data preparation choices well upstream of the model itself. | 4 | Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Embedding, Vector Database | Microsoft Learn, Google Cloud, NVIDIA |
| 46 | Data & Knowledge | Knowledge Base | A structured, curated repository of an organisation's information (policies, products, procedures, FAQs) that AI systems draw on to answer questions accurately. A well-maintained knowledge base is the fuel for reliable enterprise AI: models are only as good as the sources they are grounded in. For executives, this reframes AI value as dependent on knowledge management: investment in clean, current, well-governed content pays off directly in AI quality. Neglected or contradictory knowledge bases produce confident, wrong answers. Knowledge readiness is often the real bottleneck to AI adoption, not the model. | 6 | Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Enterprise Search, Knowledge Graph | Microsoft Learn, IBM, Google Cloud |
| 47 | Data & Knowledge | Knowledge Graph | A structured network representing entities (people, products, places) and the relationships between them, giving AI an explicit, machine-readable map of how things connect. Knowledge graphs add precision and reasoning that free text alone lacks, and increasingly complement LLMs by grounding them in verified facts and relationships. For executives, they matter where accuracy and traceability are critical, such as compliance, supply chain, or customer 360 views. Combining knowledge graphs with generative AI (sometimes called GraphRAG) can reduce hallucination and improve explainability, at the cost of the effort to build and maintain the graph. | 5 | Ontology & Taxonomy, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Semantic Layer | Google Cloud, IBM, NVIDIA |
| 48 | Data & Knowledge | Enterprise Search | Technology that lets employees find information across an organisation's many systems and documents from one place, now transformed by semantic and generative AI. Modern enterprise search understands questions, retrieves by meaning, and can summarise answers with sources. ## Decision Memory

Decision: Adopt a rebalanced 138-concept literacy set (from a 136-item technical-heavy draft) as the canonical source for C-level Enterprise AI literacy, with 12 categories.

Context: The original list was comprehensive but skewed technical and light on the economics, adoption, and governance vocabulary boards actually debate. It also contained overlaps and granular items below C-level relevance.

Alternatives: (a) Keep the 136 items as-is; (b) Add new terms without cutting (approaching 150); (c) Full rebalance. Full rebalance was chosen for maximum executive relevance.

Rationale: A C-suite literacy set should prioritise decision-useful concepts. We added value and adoption terms (AI ROI, TCO, Build vs Buy, Use-Case Prioritization, Time-to-Value, AI Literacy, Shadow AI, Change Management, Reskilling, Citizen Developer), current technical terms (Test-Time Compute, Chain-of-Thought, Structured Outputs, Prompt Caching, Computer Use, Deep Research, LoRA/PEFT, GPU, MoE, Reasoning Model), and governance depth (Bias & Fairness, Model Card, AI-BOM, Confidential Computing, Copyright & IP, EU AI Act tiers). We consolidated granular items (vector index into Vector Database; short/long-term memory into Agent Memory; prompt versioning and registry into Prompt Management; ontology and taxonomy merged; zero-shot and few-shot merged) and de-duplicated (Reasoning kept as Reasoning Model; Grounding distinguished from RAG).

Consequences: 138 concepts, each with an executive definition, importance score, related concepts, and web-verified authoritative sources. This file is the single source of truth; the enterprise AI concepts wordcloud is regenerated from it. Newer and contested terms were web-verified in July 2026; definitions reflect that point in time and should be reviewed as the field and regulation evolve.
